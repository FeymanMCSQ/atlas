## Post 1

**Content:**
We bootstrapped FeedbackPanda to $55,000 MRR and sold it for a life-changing amount.
But the truth behind the success was darker: I was completely burned out.

I was the single point of failure for everything technical. I worked 80-hour weeks. I answered support tickets at 3 AM. By the time we exited, I couldn't even look at code without feeling physical anxiety. 

Hustle culture tells you to push through the pain. Reality tells you that if you build a machine that requires you to operate it 24/7, you haven't built a business. You've built a prison.

Build processes before you need them. Delegate before you break.

The "Audience-First" approach to building a SaaS in 2024:
Do not write a single line of code until you have talked to 50 people with the problem.

1. Find an established community (a subreddit, a Slack group, a Facebook group).
2. Become a helpful, contributing member. DO NOT self-promote.
3. Observe the problems they constantly complain about.
4. Validate a solution with them openly.
5. Build exactly what they ask for.

If you build for an audience you already have access to, getting your first 100 customers is a conversation, not a marketing campaign.

**Content:**
"Build in public" isn't about bragging about your MRR. 
It's about sharing your vulnerabilities. It's about posting the failed database migration. It's about talking about the marketing campaign that completely flopped.

When you only post your wins, you look like a corporate brand. 
When you post your failures, you look like a human being. People buy from humans they trust, not perfectly polished corporate facades.

Share the messy middle of your entrepreneurial journey.

## Post 4
**Likes:** 11,500
**Content:**
Niche down until it hurts.
When we launched FeedbackPanda, we didn't build "productivity software for teachers." That's too broad. 
We built "productivity software for online ESL teachers working for VIPKid."

By focusing on a hyper-specific niche:
- Our marketing copy wrote itself.
- We knew exactly where they hung out online.
- They immediately understood the value proposition.

When you try to be everything to everyone, you end up being nothing to no one. Niche down to a tiny, passionate audience.

## Post 5
**Likes:** 10,200
**Content:**
Bootstrapping a SaaS is entirely different from running a VC-funded startup.
A funded startup is a race car. It burns fuel (cash) at incredible speeds to reach a massive end goal, but it will crash if it runs out of gas.

A bootstrapped business is a sailboat. You have to learn how to catch the wind. You move slower, but you use the resources around you (customer revenue) to propel you indefinitely. You don't need a billion-dollar destination. 

Stop reading TechCrunch and expecting your journey to look like a unicorn's. Embrace the sailboat.
